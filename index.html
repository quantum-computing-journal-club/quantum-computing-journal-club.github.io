Welcome!

This journal club is dedicated to presenting and discussing the latest advancements in the field of quantum computing, both from theoretical and experimental perspectives. The idea of quantum computation is not new. It dates back to the 1980s physicists faced an exponential increase in overhead when simulating quantum dynamics, and prompted Yuri Manin and Richard Feynman to independently suggest that a hardware capable of directly exploit quantum phenomena, might be more efficient. In the following years, many quantum algorithms has been proposed, for example Deutsch's algorithm in 1985 or Simon's algorithm in 1994. These algorithms don't solve practical problems, but demonstrate the superiority that one could gain by exploiting quantum properties as superposition. A more famous and relevant algorithm is Shor's algorithm, proposed back in 1994 by Peter Shor, that breaks the widely used RSA encryption protocols.

Since then, the field of quantum computing has evolved through an extensive theoretical research. However, only in recent years the technological advancements enabled reliable experimental implementations, bringing us closer (although still very, very, very, very far from a sufficient amount of reliable error-corrected qubits) to the practical realization of quantum computers.

The goal of this journal club is to explore new computational paradigms, new implementations, and more. Depending on the topic, prior knowledge might or might not be expected. For more, see Background Material.

Organized by Giovanni Cemin.

Format

We meet every Wednesdays at 13:00 (Dresden local time) in Room 2A1 at MPI-PKS. If you are not in Dresden and your don't want to miss out all the fun, meetings are streamed on Zoom. An email announcement is sent before each session with the paper, presenter, Zoom link and any changes to the meeting room.
